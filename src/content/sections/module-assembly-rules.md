The module is not a building — it is a building system. Its value lies not in the individual unit but in the rules governing how units combine.

## Connection Types

Three connection details define how modules join:

1. **Roof-to-roof** — pitched roofs meet at a gutter channel. Water is captured at every connection, making the building wetter (more water-secure) as it grows.
2. **Gutter channels** — the space between modules is not waste space but infrastructure. Volcanic gravel filtration in the gutter cleans rainwater before it enters sub-grade tanks.
3. **Wall-column joints** — informed by Hawaiian vernacular joinery. Mass timber connections that can be assembled, disassembled, and reassembled — critical for repair after future events.

## Aggregation Rules

Modules aggregate following these principles:

1. **Field condition** — no front, no back, no center. The building grows as a mat, not a tower.
2. **Topographic response** — modules step, slide, and terrace in response to Lahaina's gentle coastal slope. No site flattening required.
3. **Courtyard network** — every cluster of modules creates an outdoor room. These courtyards are the social heart of the building — gathering, play, ventilation, and daylighting.
4. **Distributed clusters** — Learning clusters, Public clusters, Service clusters each have their own character but connect through the courtyard network.
5. **Circulation as web** — there are no corridors. Movement happens through courtyards, under covered walkways between modules, and along the edges of the field. Getting lost is part of learning.
6. **Landscape integration** — the building does not sit on the landscape but grows from it. Stormwater flows through, not around.
7. **Growth and repairability** — modules can be added at any edge. Damaged modules can be replaced without demolishing neighbors. The system is designed for a 100-year timeline of adaptation.

## What This Means in Practice

Three modules in a row create a classroom wing. A U-shape of modules around a courtyard creates a residential compound. Dozens of modules aggregated with varying roof heights and courtyard sizes create the elementary school. The same grammar produces housing, community centers, and health clinics.

The module is explicitly designed as a prototype — not a finished product but a replicable unit that communities can adapt to their own needs. The thesis argues that imposing fixed designs on displaced communities reproduces the power dynamics that created vulnerability. The module offers a grammar, not a sentence.
