The recovery plan is organized around four buffer zones that work together to protect the town, manage water, and create space for community life.

**Coastal Buffer** — Defined by sea level rise projections and bounded by Wainee Street, this zone implements a strategy of partial retreat. Rather than armoring the shoreline, the plan creates a coastal park and promenade that provides public access, protects heritage sites, and allows the coastline to shift naturally over time. Front Street transforms from a commercial corridor into a pedestrian promenade connecting cultural sites and community spaces.

**Riparian Buffer** — Along Lahaina's streams (Kaua'ula, Kahoma, Kanahā), the plan restores natural corridors. Decades of channelization and development encroachment have stripped these waterways of their ecological and flood-protection functions. The riparian buffer re-vegetates stream banks, creates public access trails, and allows brackish water at stream mouths to form natural wetland habitat.

**Peri-Urban Buffer** — Between the proposed boulevard and the highway, an agricultural transition zone serves as both a firebreak and a productive landscape. This irrigated agricultural park uses captured mountain water to maintain green, fire-resistant vegetation while producing food for local consumption. It also provides a buffer between the highway's wildland-urban interface and the residential core.

**Water Collection Hinterland** — Above the highway, the plan reinforces the historic ditch system to capture mountain rainfall before it runs off unused. Two collection zones — upper mountain and mid-level — feed water into the agricultural park and ultimately into the town's water supply, reducing dependence on the stressed aquifer.
