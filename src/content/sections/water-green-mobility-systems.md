The systems trace the evolution of Lahaina's core infrastructure from pre-fire conditions through post-disaster dysfunction to proposed recovery configurations. Five interconnected systems structure this analysis: water, mobility, re-densification, network of nodes, and green infrastructure. Each system is examined through a before-after-proposed sequence that documents existing conditions, diagnoses failures exposed by the fire, and proposes interventions aligned with the design principles established in Section 3C.

The systems do not operate independently. Water management shapes the location and character of green infrastructure. Mobility networks define the edges and access points of the re-densification zone. Community nodes anchor the intersection of multiple systems, concentrating services, gathering spaces, and infrastructure in hybrid facilities that serve daily needs while providing emergency capacity.

## Water

Water is the central organizing system of the recovery framework. The 2023 fire was not mitigated because water could not be delivered where it was needed — a failure of infrastructure, not of resources. Abundant rainfall in the West Maui Mountains goes uncaptured, while the municipal system lacks redundancy, storage, and emergency capacity. Addressing this failure requires restructuring water management from a centralized utility into a distributed network integrated with the urban fabric.

**Before Fire.** The town relied on the municipal supply, irrigation systems, and surface water channels. They used historic ditches and streams that came from the plantation-era engineering. For the most part, this system functioned adequately for daily functions. But it was vulnerable if you looked closer. Drought and saltwater intrusion to start. Also, if infrastructure was damaged, there wasn't really a backup plan to fix things quickly. Historic plantation-era ditch network was originally designed to move water from the West Maui Mountains to the sugarcane fields. But after the agricultural decline, people abandoned those ditches. This left significant conveyance capacity unused.

**After Fire.** Lahaina had a big issue because they depended on a single-source distribution network. They had minimal backup capacity. There was also a lack of compartmentalization to isolate damaged areas, which is a major problem. The plantation-era ditches intended for irrigation were disconnected from the emergency supply strategies. They were pretty much unusable for firefighting in the key zones. Another issue was the damage to exposed above-ground pipes, pump stations, and vulnerable storage tanks. When they did post-fire assessments, inconsistencies in maintenance records, unclear jurisdictional responsibilities, and absence of a coordinated emergency water management plan were revealed.

**The Proposed Water System** works in two zones. First, there is Zone One in the hinterland. The main idea here is to catch a large volume of rainfall. They plan to do this by rehabilitated ditch segments, using collection ponds, and intake structures. It's set up to gravity-fed supply, so it is independent of electrical pumping to work. Zone Two takes runoff water from the peri-urban buffer and sends it toward the agricultural park. There are retention basins to intercept flow before it enters the town. This is allowing deployment for irrigation, aquifer recharge, and emergency reserves. The three main intervention typologies start with runoff capture which happens at the building and parcel scale. Then, there is interception along the Highway 30 corridor. This is where the upland flow concentrates, so it makes sense to put it there. Finally, they use collection ponds at the topographic low points in the hinterland and peri-urban zones.

## Green Infrastructure

Green infrastructure means putting ecological systems back into cities, helping manage water, providing shade, and creating habitat.

**Before Fire.** Before the fire, Lahaina didn't have much green infrastructure. Fragmented vegetation existed along streets and rivers. The historic areas had some parks that stood out, but they didn't really help with stormwater management. The green spaces didn't connect well enough to give full climatic benefits. Plants were pretty much in isolated patches instead of being spread out everywhere.

**After Fire.** Most green spaces were removed from the wildfires. Now there is slow growth in some of the remaining ecology.

**Proposed Green Network.** The coastal promenade forms the border of this green area. People walk there, and you can see vegetated buffers along the waterfront. Street trees and coastal plantings provide shade for people walking. Plus, they cool down the microclimate, which is great on hot days. It's not just about making it look nice, but also about creating a comfortable spot to hang out. The promenade ties together some heritage sites, like the Banyan Tree, Mokuʻula, and a few historic churches. They're all joined by a shaded corridor where you can walk.

The Riparian Corridor runs along Kahoma Stream and goes from the peri-urban buffer all the way to the coastal discharge point. The primary ecological spine in the redensification zone. This sets up a habitat corridor for animals and lets stormwater move naturally. The riparian park has public access, mostly near ditches and ponds. This keeps ecological health while letting folks enjoy it.

The agricultural edge is the peri-urban buffer between the busy boulevard and Highway 30. It is an ecological transition. Irrigation in the agricultural area helps keep the landscape fuel moisture high. This makes it less likely for fires to start and is good for local food and groundwater recharge. Also, the border area provides visual and ecological transition from the re-densified core to more open spaces.

## Mobility

**Before Fire.** Before the fire, the way people moved around Lahaina was organized along this coastal corridor. Front Street was the primary artery for everything. Leisure, eating, gathering and walking. The streets were designed to prioritize cars above all else. Even then, it didn't work well because there were limited alternative routes to drive inland. Because of these missing routes, traffic was immobile. Peak tourist and commuter periods, led to grid lock. The sidewalk networks remain to be hit or miss and bike lane networks barely exist. Plus, if you wanted connectivity from one neighborhood to another, you usually had to deal with high-traffic roads just to get through. Down near the water, the streets networks tight and encouraged for walking, but the hills and old property lines constrained movement. That network is adequate for low-density residential access, but it turns catastrophic if rapid evacuation is needed.

**After Fire.** Dead-end streets turned into trap points. Residents trying to flee got funneled back toward routes they impassable while the fire got closer. Looking back, assessments showed that things like wayfinding failures, inadequate lighting, and unclear right-of-way management made the structural problem worse. The lesson here was unambiguous. A mobility system that is designed just for daily convenience can't really accommodate it when emergency mass movement. It just doesn't work when exit route needs to be function at the same time.

**Proposed Mobility.** The new mobility framework centers on Highway 30. It converted and is now called the New Boulevard. You can think of this as the central spine for Lahaina since all the major access routes connect right into it. The system is designed for redundancy, too. This ensures that neighborhoods, service hubs, and the re-densified core maintain access in emergencies. It is diverting heavy traffic away from the waterfront. Also, the Mauka Highway extension provides a regional bypass capacity above the town. This allows through-traffic to circumvent Lahaina entirely. That will reduce vehicle load on the internal streets.

The most defining part of this framework is how it addresses the dead-end problem. Before, streets terminated, but now every single one is connected to the arterial spine. This removes trap points that contributed for the evacuation failures during the 2023 fire. Because of network restructuring, things are much safer. Now, any point inside the re-densification zone has at least two independent egress routes to the boulevard or the coastal promenade. This preventing the single-exit vulnerability, which proved fatal in the past.
