## Academic Contribution

This thesis advances post-disaster urbanism in three ways.

First, it demonstrates that water infrastructure and fire resilience are the same problem. The four-buffer framework treats water capture, firebreaks, coastal protection, and housing as an integrated system — not separate engineering challenges. This reframes recovery from "rebuild what was lost" to "build what was always needed."

Second, it positions the architectural module as a recovery tool. The 26×26-foot unit is not a house — it is a grammar. It can become a classroom, a clinic, a home, or a shelter depending on how modules are assembled. This challenges the post-disaster housing paradigm of identical replacement units imposed by agencies. The module gives communities agency over their own reconstruction.

Third, it integrates indigenous land management with contemporary infrastructure. The ahupuaʻa is not a metaphor in this thesis — it is an operational framework. Mountain-to-coast water connectivity, integrated resource management, and community-scale governance are not romantic ideals but engineering strategies with pre-colonial precedent.

## What Remains

This thesis is a beginning, not an end. What needs to happen next:

- **Structural engineering** — The module system needs load testing, connection verification, and fire-resistance certification for mass timber in Hawaiʻi's regulatory context
- **Hydrological modeling** — Water capture volumes need to be modeled against actual rainfall data and demand projections to quantify how much the distributed system reduces aquifer dependence
- **Community engagement** — The framework needs to be presented to displaced Lahaina residents, community organizations, and County planners for feedback, critique, and adaptation
- **Cost analysis** — Infrastructure rehabilitation (ditches, reservoirs, roads) needs cost estimation and phasing tied to federal aid timelines (FEMA, CDBG-DR, HUD)
- **Policy advocacy** — Anti-gentrification measures need legislative champions and community organizing to become law, not just design recommendations
- **GIS data sharing** — The spatial analysis underlying this thesis should be published as open data for other researchers and planners to build upon

This website is part of that next step. By making the research, maps, analysis, and proposals publicly accessible, the thesis moves from academic document to community resource. The people of Lahaina are the ultimate judges of whether this framework serves their needs.
