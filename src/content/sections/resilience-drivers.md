This section examines global post-disaster recovery precedents. New Orleans after Hurricane Katrina (2005) demonstrated both the failures and innovations of large-scale recovery — from the X-Codes system for rapid building assessment to the $14.5 billion levee system rebuilt by the Army Corps of Engineers. The Make It Right Foundation's Lower Ninth Ward project showed how architectural innovation could serve displaced communities, while also revealing the limitations of top-down intervention.

The 1995 Kobe earthquake in Japan offered a different model: parks and open spaces serving as emergency shelters, with pre-planned evacuation routes and community gathering points built into the urban fabric before disaster struck. Kobe's recovery demonstrated that resilience infrastructure must be designed into the everyday landscape, not added after catastrophe.

Chile's incremental housing model (Elemental/Aravena) provides precedent for phased construction that empowers residents to complete their own homes over time — a model particularly relevant to Lahaina's displaced community seeking agency in their own recovery.
