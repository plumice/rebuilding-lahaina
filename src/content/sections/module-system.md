At the smallest scale, the thesis proposes a 26-by-26-foot building module as the basic unit of construction. This module draws on two traditions: the Hawaiian hale (traditional house) and plantation-era timber construction.

The module uses mass timber construction — stronger and more fire-resistant than the light-frame wood that was destroyed in the wildfire. Hawaiian vernacular joinery techniques inform the connection details, creating a structural system that is both culturally resonant and technically sound.

Each module is designed to capture water. The roof slopes direct rainfall into gutters between connected modules, where volcanic gravel filtration cleans the water before it flows into sub-grade storage tanks. This building-scale water capture supplements the municipal supply and reduces demand on the stressed aquifer.

Solar response is built into the module's orientation and shading strategy. Lahaina faces west, receiving intense afternoon sun. Deep overhangs, adjustable screens, and ventilation paths are designed into each module to reduce cooling loads without mechanical systems.

Modules aggregate into larger buildings by connecting along their edges. Three modules in a row create a classroom wing. A cluster of modules around a courtyard creates a residential compound. The school building is an aggregation of dozens of modules, demonstrating how a simple unit can produce complex, humane architecture at community scale.

The module is explicitly designed as a prototype — a replicable unit that communities can adapt and build upon, not a fixed product imposed from outside.
