**Theoretical Framework**

This thesis positions itself within the emerging field of post-disaster urbanism — the study and design of cities following catastrophic events, with focus on resilience, equity, and sustainable development. Three theoretical streams inform the approach.

First, ecological resilience theory. C.S. Holling's foundational work (1973) established that resilience is not about returning to a previous state but about a system's capacity to absorb disturbance and reorganize while retaining essential function. Brian Walker and David Salt extended this to social-ecological systems, arguing that communities and ecosystems are coupled — you cannot rebuild one without addressing the other. For Lahaina, this means recovery cannot simply reconstruct the pre-fire town. The pre-fire town was the vulnerability.

Second, integrated water urbanism. The Waggonner & Ball Greater New Orleans Urban Water Plan (2013) demonstrated that water infrastructure can serve as public amenity and resilience strategy simultaneously. Iñaki Alday's work — the Zaragoza Water Park and the Yamuna River Project — established methodologies for treating water corridors as design opportunities rather than engineering problems. The concept of "daily resilience" from Zaragoza is central: landscapes that serve everyday recreational and social functions are maintained through use and remain functional during extreme events. Landscapes reserved solely for emergency function degrade through neglect.

Third, indigenous land management. Kamanamaikalani Beamer's work on the ahupuaʻa system provides both a philosophical framework and practical strategies. The ahupuaʻa organized land from mountain peak to ocean — an integrated management system where water flowed mauka to makai, sustaining agriculture and community. This is not historical nostalgia but operational precedent: the thesis proposes a recovery that restores mountain-to-coast water connectivity, echoing ahupuaʻa logic with contemporary infrastructure.

**Research Methodology**

The thesis employs an iterative design process moving through four phases:

1. **Diagnosis** — Multi-scalar analysis from regional to node scale. GIS analysis using USGS Digital Elevation Models, Rainfall Atlas data, NOAA sea level rise projections, and County of Maui zoning and damage assessment data. This phase produces the vulnerability maps and system overlays.

2. **Projection** — Precedent-based reasoning drawing on New Orleans, Kobe, Chile, Medellín, and other recovery contexts. Not direct transplantation — each precedent is analyzed for transferable principles, not copied forms. The four-buffer framework emerged from overlaying vulnerability analysis with precedent strategies.

3. **Implementation** — Design development at site and node scales. The elementary school prototype, module system, and street designs are tested against the diagnostic findings. Does the school serve its neighborhood? Does the module capture water? Does the street network eliminate dead ends?

4. **Synthesis** — Evaluation against resilience criteria: does the proposal reduce vulnerability to fire, flood, and drought? Does it serve displaced residents equitably? Does it respect and integrate Hawaiian cultural practices? The resilience evaluation matrix provides a systematic framework for this assessment.

This methodology draws on Alday and Gupta's "Urban Layers" analytical approach from the Yamuna River Project — moving between regional diagnosis and site-specific intervention iteratively, rather than designing top-down from a master plan.

The approach is explicitly not a master plan. It is a framework — a set of principles, systems, and prototypes that communities can adapt. The module is a prototype, not a prescription. The buffer framework defines zones, not buildings. This distinction matters: top-down reconstruction imposed on displaced communities reproduces the power dynamics that created vulnerability in the first place.
