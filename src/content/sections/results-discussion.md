The thesis proposed five hypotheses. Here is how the design work addresses each.

**H1: Multi-scale recovery integrating water, green infrastructure, and housing can rebuild while addressing vulnerabilities.** The four-buffer framework demonstrates this at every scale — from regional water capture to building-scale cisterns, from the agricultural firebreak to the modular construction system. The cumulative systems plan shows all layers working together. The critical test is water: the distributed capture strategy reduces aquifer dependence but does not eliminate the 41.8% deficit alone. Full recovery requires both infrastructure rehabilitation and policy change.

**H2: Cultural identity strengthens social cohesion and resilience.** The module system draws on Hawaiian hale traditions. The coastal promenade connects heritage sites. Mokuʻula restoration integrates cultural preservation with water infrastructure. The ahupuaʻa concept structures the entire mountain-to-coast framework. However, cultural integration is easiest to claim and hardest to verify — the real test is whether displaced Native Hawaiian residents recognize their culture in the rebuilt environment, which requires ongoing community engagement beyond the scope of a thesis.

**H3: Global precedents provide actionable strategies.** New Orleans informed the equity framework (anti-displacement, community land trusts). Kobe informed the dual-use hub strategy (parks as shelters). Chile's incremental housing model informed the module system's growth-and-repair logic. The Yamuna River Project and Zaragoza Water Park informed the water-as-public-space approach. No precedent was transplanted wholesale — each was filtered through Lahaina's specific conditions.

**H4: Equitable policies can prevent disaster gentrification.** The thesis identifies mechanisms (community land trusts, inclusionary zoning, right-of-first-refusal, anti-speculation measures) but cannot guarantee political implementation. The 83.2% renter-occupied rate and ongoing TVR pressure make this the most precarious hypothesis — design alone cannot prevent gentrification without policy enforcement.

**H5: Participatory methodology produces better outcomes.** The iterative diagnosis-projection-implementation-synthesis methodology allowed the design to respond to findings rather than impose predetermined solutions. However, true community participation requires more than a thesis timeline allows. The framework is designed to be adapted through ongoing community input.

## Limitations

This is a design thesis, not an implemented project. Key limitations include: no structural engineering verification of the module system, no hydrological modeling of water capture volumes, no cost estimation for infrastructure rehabilitation, and no community engagement process beyond initial conversations and site visits. The thesis offers a framework and prototypes, not construction documents.

## Scalability

The module system and buffer framework are explicitly designed for adaptation beyond Lahaina. Any coastal community facing wildfire, water scarcity, and displacement could apply the diagnostic methodology and buffer logic to their own conditions. The module's grammar — connect, cluster, grow — is site-independent.
