Lahaina's history is written in its landscape. Before Western contact, the ahupua'a system organized land from mountain peak to ocean — an integrated management system where water flowed mauka (mountain) to makai (sea), sustaining agriculture and community.

In the 1820s, Lahaina served as the capital of the Hawaiian Kingdom, and missionary-era construction introduced new building materials. The Lanakila Church and other coral-and-stone structures from the 1860s still defined the town's character before the fire.

Around 1901, the Pioneer Mill Company transformed West Maui's water landscape. An expansive network of ditches, reservoirs, and pipes was built to irrigate sugarcane. By 1935, electric pumps were irrigating over 10,000 acres. When the mill closed in 1999, the irrigation stopped — but the water diversions remained in place, starving the landscape of moisture.

Statehood in 1959 triggered rapid tourism development. Lahaina shifted from an agricultural to a tourism-dependent economy, concentrating commercial activity along Front Street and increasing vulnerability to both economic disruption and physical disaster.

The August 2023 wildfire destroyed approximately 2,200 homes and displaced 6,000-7,000 residents, eliminating historic sites and infrastructure that had defined the town for generations.
