The elementary school is the thesis's most developed design intervention — a demonstration of how the module system, community hub concept, and Hawaiian cultural values work together at architectural scale.

## Mat-Building Precedents

The school draws on a lineage of mat-building — architecture organized as a horizontal field of connected spaces rather than a singular monumental form. Alison Smithson coined the term in 1974, identifying buildings that "can be cut and added to without changing their essential nature."

Key precedents informing the design:

- **Aldo van Eyck's Amsterdam Orphanage (1960)** — repeated rooms and courtyards with no hierarchical center, creating spatial equality for children
- **Charles Correa's Gandhi Ashram Museum (1963)** — square modules with shaded verandas, cultural capacity for ritual and gathering in tropical climate
- **Herman Hertzberger's Montessori Schools (1970s-80s)** — non-hierarchical learning environments where circulation IS the classroom, thresholds become gathering spaces
- **St. Xavier's Primary School** — classrooms and shared spaces as distributed system with semi-open thresholds

Five architectural rules emerge from these precedents:

1. **Aggregation over monumentality** — horizontal field, not singular object
2. **Environmental responsiveness** — courtyards, verandas, shaded paths, variable roof heights
3. **Distributed program** — spatial equalization and redundancy
4. **Ground plane as primary organizer** — circulation embedded, boundaries between classroom/corridor/courtyard blurred
5. **Incrementalism** — repair, expansion, adaptation without full reconstruction

## Program Requirements

The school serves 200-300 students and includes:

- Administration offices: 3,000-4,000 sq ft
- Cafeteria (multipurpose): 5,000-7,000 sq ft
- Classrooms K-5: 600-800 sq ft each
- Shared learning spaces: 1,000-2,000 sq ft
- Library/media center: 2,000-3,000 sq ft
- Gymnasium: 8,000-10,000 sq ft
- Outdoor courtyards: 1,500-3,000 sq ft each
- Health services and first response offices

## Floor Plan Logic

The ground floor organizes into four zones, each built from 26x26-foot modules:

1. **Public Access / Health / First Response** — the primary neighborhood interface, directly accessible from the boulevard. During emergencies, this zone becomes the command center.
2. **Cafeteria / Entrance** — central multipurpose space with outdoor courtyard overflow. Passive ventilation through the module roof system.
3. **Classroom Wing** — porous transition modules create informal learning spaces. Natural daylight enters through gaps between module roofs.
4. **Gymnasium** — positioned at the edge for large-span flexibility. Dual access: public entry for community use, and direct boulevard connection for emergency shelter conversion.

## Emergency Conversion

The school's everyday and emergency functions are designed as one system, not two:

- The gymnasium converts to emergency shelter (sleeping, supplies, medical triage)
- The cafeteria serves as community feeding station
- Health offices become first-response triage
- Classrooms serve as family housing units
- Boulevard access allows ambulance staging without disrupting residential streets

This dual-use approach draws from Kobe's post-earthquake lesson: resilience infrastructure that serves everyday life is maintained through daily use. Facilities designed only for emergencies degrade from neglect.
