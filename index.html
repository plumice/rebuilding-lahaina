00 — Abstract
Thesis Statement
The 2023 wildfire in Lahaina revealed critical vulnerabilities in infrastructure, water management, and disaster preparedness. Lack of water to control the fires, poor accessibility to certain areas of the city and other dysfunctionalities ended in one of the most catastrophic events in Hawaiian history.

Currently there is no clear route map for the rebuilding of the city nor its adaptation to climate events and their increasing frequency and violence. This thesis proposes recovery through a multi-scale plan that includes the major urban systems, the relation of the urban fabric with the coast and the hinterland, and a sequence of strategic nodes to serve the communities. It integrates water systems, slow mobility, and human-centered infrastructure to create a resilient and adaptive framework for Lahaina’s long-term redevelopment.

By holistically targeting these systemic weaknesses through scalable site-specific interventions, the model challenges conventional fragmented disaster recovery paradigms and sets a new footprint for urban resilience, adaptation and post-disaster recovery.
Research Questions

1. How can Lahaina’s recovery address systemic vulnerabilities in infrastructure, water management, and housing to ensure resilience against future disasters?

2. What role can cultural and historical identity play in shaping Lahaina’s recovery framework?

3. How can global precedents (New Orleans post-Katrina, Kobe 1995) inform Lahaina’s recovery strategies?

4. How can recovery efforts balance land ownership, zoning, and economic disparities to avoid disaster gentrification?

5. What methodologies can best integrate community participation, stakeholder engagement, and technical expertise?

04 — Key Terms
Definitions & Glossary
Core terminology used throughout the thesis.

Adaptive Recovery

Framework for rebuilding that incorporates flexibility and resilience to adapt to future climate, social, and economic changes.

Ahupua’a

Traditional Hawaiian land divisions extending from mountains to sea, integrating natural water systems for agriculture and community sustenance.

Cultural Resilience

A community’s ability to preserve and adapt its cultural identity, traditions, and social structures in the face of adversity.

Disaster Gentrification

Post-disaster recovery processes that disproportionately benefit wealthier or external actors, leading to community displacement.

Integrated Water Systems

Holistic water management combining flood control, ecological preservation, and community accessibility.

Iterative Design

Cyclical design methodology emphasizing continuous refinement through feedback, testing, and evaluation.

Node-Based Planning

Urban design creating interconnected functional and symbolic spaces as catalysts for community cohesion and resilience.

Permeable Frameworks

Urban systems designed to manage water sustainably by incorporating natural and built environments.

Post-Disaster Urbanism

Study and design of cities following catastrophic events, focusing on resilience, equity, and sustainable development.

Resilient Housing

Housing designed to withstand environmental stressors and adapt to future risks through modular and sustainable approaches.

Slow Mobility Networks

Transportation systems prioritizing pedestrians, cyclists, and public transit to reduce environmental impact.

Social Cohesion

The strength of relationships and solidarity within a community, fostered through inclusive planning.

Vulnerability Cycles

Recurring patterns of risk and recovery failure caused by systemic weaknesses in infrastructure, planning, and governance.

05 — Timeline
Lahaina — Key Events
Major spatial and infrastructural events that shaped the town.
Pre-Contact

Ahupua’a System

Traditional Hawaiian land management from mountain to sea; integrated water systems for agriculture and community.

1820s

Royal Capital & Missionary Settlement

Lahaina serves as capital of the Hawaiian Kingdom; missionary-era construction introduces new building materials.

~1860s

Lanakila Church & Coral Construction

Coral and stone cobble construction (AR 24) links to this era; pre-industrial water infrastructure.

~1901

Pioneer Mill Water Infrastructure

Expansive network of ditches, reservoirs, and pipes developed; electric pumps irrigating 10,000+ acres by 1935.

1959

Statehood & Tourism Transformation

Hawai’i statehood triggers rapid tourism development; Lahaina shifts from agricultural to tourism-dependent economy.

June 2022

Lahaina Aquifer Sector Designated

CWRM designates entire sector as Surface and Ground Water Management Area; state-level control imposed.

August 2023

Wildfire Devastation

Fire destroys ~2,200 homes, displaces 6,000–7,000 residents, eliminates historic sites and infrastructure.

Sept 2025

Stage 2 Water Shortage Declared

Demand exceeds reliable capacity by 41.8%; new water meters cannot be processed, blocking reconstruction.

2026 (Projected)

Kahana Well Online

New well expected to reduce deficit to ~15.7% — still insufficient for full recovery demand.
